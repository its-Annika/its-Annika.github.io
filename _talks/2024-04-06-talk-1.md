This study aims to show that *Löffelsprache*, a German *Geheimsprache* or secret language cannot be accurately classified using existing criteria associated with the description of language games. Speakers describe the process of creating *Löffelsprache* forms as follows: after every vowel, add <lew>, and then repeat the vowel: *sechs* ‘six’, becomes *selewechs*. Two language game analyses applicable to *Löffelsprache* are affixation (Bagemihl, 1987; McCarthy, 1982; Pound, 1963), and a two-part analysis which aims to further develop the concept of affixation, namely, imposition and insertion (Botne and Davis, 2000). Both affixation and imposition rely on consistent vowel quality on either side of the language game sequence, through vowel copying or nucleus splitting respectively, while insertion requires the language game sequence to be fully specified. Forms such as *geht* [get], [gɪ.lɪ.vet], *wie* [vi], [vɪ.lɪ.vi], and *dir* [diɐ], [dɪ.lɪ.viɐ], demonstrate that none of these analyses account for *Löffelsprache*, as changes in vowel quality rule out vowel copying and nucleus splitting, and the presence of a vowel on either side of <lew> disqualifies insertion. To account for *Löffelsprache*’s behavior, I propose iterative templatic transformation: iterative in that a process of adding new phonetic material occurs in relation to every nucleus of the base word, and templatic in that every *Löffelsprache* form must match a specified output structure. Instead of inserting or affixing a sequence into every syllable, *Löffelsprache* transforms each syllable of the base word into a three-syllable foot anapest foot, where the base nucleus appears in the stressed third syllable of the foot. Additional stipulations built into the template fully explain all *Löffelsprache* forms.
